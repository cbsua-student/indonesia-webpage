Language Background

Lesson 01

Indonesian language or Bahasa Indonesia is the official language of the Republic of Indonesia. The root of this language is Malay which belongs to the Austronesian language family. Malay has been widely used for centuries as a lingua franca in the archipelago.

In addition to Malay, Indonesian vocabulary is heavily influenced by other local languages such as Javanese, Minangkabau, and Sundanese. Indonesian also borrows words from foreign languages such as English, Arabic, Dutch, Sanskrit, and Portuguese. Bahasa Indonesia is primarily used in education, administration, and media, so in modern times, every Indonesian speaks the language.

‼️ Major local languages in Indonesia

Most of these languages are part of the Austronesian language family and Papuan languages.

Grammar Overview

Lesson 02
💡 Indonesian grammar is considered one of the easiest in Asia. No tones, no verb conjugation, and no grammatical gender — just clear patterns and time words.

Basic Word Order

Pattern: Subject + Verb + Object (SVO), similar to English. Adjectives always follow the noun.

Saya makan nasi. = I eat rice.
rumah besar = big house

Questions and Negation

Use apakah or rising intonation for questions. Tidak negates verbs and adjectives. Bukan negates nouns.

Apakah kamu suka kopi? = Do you like coffee?
Saya tidak suka teh. = I don't like tea.
Ini bukan buku. = This is not a book.

Plural Forms

Repeat the noun to indicate plural (reduplication). A number already present makes repetition unnecessary. Beberapa (some) or banyak (many) also work.

jeruk = orange
jeruk-jeruk = oranges
dua jeruk = two oranges

Verb Tense Pattern

Verbs remain unchanged regardless of subject or tense. Time adverbs carry the tense meaning.

sudah/telah makan = already ate
sedang belajar = is studying
akan pergi = will go

Inclusive vs. Exclusive "We"

Indonesian distinguishes between "we" that includes the listener and "we" that does not. There are no gendered pronouns.

kita = we (including listener)
kami = we (excluding listener)
dia = he, she, or it

Special Features

Classifiers are used before nouns (like buah for objects). Loanwords come from Dutch, Arabic, Sanskrit, and English. Reduplication also signals emphasis.

hati-hati = careful (emphasis)
buah apel = a/one apple
televisi = television (loanword)

Types of Phrases

Lesson 03
💡 In Indonesian, adjectives always follow the noun they describe. So "big house" becomes rumah besar — literally "house big." This applies to all noun phrases.

A verbal phrase is built around a verb and includes any words that complete its meaning. In Indonesian the verb often appears with time markers or negation particles.

Indonesian English Main Verb
Tidak minum Not drinking Minum
Akan berangkat Will depart Berangkat
Sedang belajar Currently studying Belajar
Akan datang Will come Datang
Belum bekerja Not yet working Bekerja

A noun phrase is built around a noun. In Bahasa Indonesia, the modifying word comes after the noun it describes.

Indonesian English Head Noun
Buku tulis Notebook Buku
Meja belajar Study desk Meja
Sepatu olahraga Sport shoes Sepatu
Lemari pakaian Wardrobe Lemari
Jam tangan Wristwatch Jam

An adjective phrase is built around an adjective with an intensifier. Common intensifiers: sangat (very), cukup (quite), sekali (very/extremely, placed after).

Indonesian English Main Adjective
Murah sekali Very cheap Murah
Sangat pintar Very smart Pintar
Cukup baik Pretty good Baik
Jauh sekali Very far Jauh
Sangat dekat Very close Dekat

A prepositional phrase links a noun to the rest of the sentence, showing location, direction, or association. Indonesian prepositions come before the noun, as in English.

Indonesian English Preposition
Ke pasar To the market Ke
Di sekolah At the school Di
Kepada orang tua To parents Kepada
Oleh kami By us Oleh
Di Jakarta In Jakarta Di

Characteristics of Phrases

Lesson 04
💡 A frasa (phrase) in Indonesian works just like in English: it is a group of words that acts as a unit, but on its own it is not a complete thought or sentence.

In Indonesian grammar, a phrase (frasa) has specific characteristics that distinguish it from single words and full sentences.

A

Must contain two or more words

A phrase is formed by combining at least two words. Example: rumah besar means "big house."

B

Must have a grammatical function

The phrase serves a role in a sentence: subject, object, complement, or adverbial of place. Example: di pasar means "at the market" and acts as an adverbial of place.

C

Must have grammatical meaning

The words together create a meaningful unit, even if not a complete thought. Example: sangat cantik means "very beautiful."

D

Must be non-predicative

A phrase does not express a full predication — no complete subject + predicate relationship. Example: buku baru means "new book" but is not a sentence.
